Research

The science behind Think2Switch™

Think2Switch™ is built on decades of peer-reviewed neuroscience. This page explains what brain-computer interfaces are, how EEG technology works, and the evidence base that underpins our approach.

The basics

What is a brain-computer interface?

A brain-computer interface (BCI) is a system that reads the electrical signals your brain naturally produces and translates them into commands that control external devices — without any physical movement.

Your brain is constantly generating tiny electrical signals — whether you're moving, thinking, focusing, or at rest. BCIs detect these signals and interpret them. The result: a direct channel between your brain and the world around you.

Think2Switch™ uses non-invasive EEG (electroencephalography) — the safest and most established BCI approach. No surgery. No implants. Just sensors that rest on the surface of the scalp.

Invasive BCIs

Require surgical implantation of electrodes directly into brain tissue. High signal fidelity, but significant medical risk. Not what Think2Switch™ uses.

Non-invasive EEG ← Think2Switch™

Sensors rest on the scalp. Safe, painless, and used in consumer and clinical settings for decades. No surgery or medical procedure required.

How it works

EEG and the Muse Athena headset

EEG measures the brain's electrical activity through sensors placed on the scalp. Different mental states — focus, relaxation, attention — produce distinct patterns that EEG can detect and distinguish.

Think2Switch™ uses the Muse Athena headset by InteraXon — a consumer EEG device with over a decade of research behind its sensor technology. The Muse platform has been used in numerous published academic studies.

During calibration, Think2Switch™ learns your personal brain signal for a specific mental action. It uses this personalised baseline to detect your intentional commands with high reliability, even accounting for natural day-to-day variation in brain signals.

Evidence base

Research informing our approach

Think2Switch™ is informed by a broad body of peer-reviewed research on EEG-based BCIs for assistive technology, motor rehabilitation, and augmentative communication.

EEG-based switch access in paediatric populations

Research demonstrates that children as young as 5–7 can reliably operate EEG-based switches with appropriate training, supporting BCI technology as an alternative access method for children with motor impairments including cerebral palsy.

BCI for ALS and progressive motor conditions

Non-invasive EEG-based BCIs have been studied extensively in populations with ALS, demonstrating that brain-based control remains possible even as physical motor function declines — providing a durable access solution across disease stages.

Safety of consumer EEG devices

Consumer EEG devices including the Muse platform have been validated in multiple published studies for both safety and signal quality. EEG is a passive measurement — it only reads signals, it does not emit anything into the brain.

We are actively conducting user research and building our clinical evidence base. If you are a researcher or clinician interested in collaborating, please get in touch.
Our approach

How we work

Possibility Neurotechnologies follows human-centred design principles throughout our research and development process. Our work is guided by co-design with users, families, and clinicians — not assumptions made in isolation.

1

Community-led design

Users, families, and OTs are involved at every stage — from identifying needs to testing solutions in real-world settings.

2

Iterative clinical testing

We conduct ongoing testing with real users — not just in controlled lab environments but in the situations where people actually live.

3

Ethical standards

All user research is conducted with full informed consent and in accordance with established ethical research standards.

4

Transparent reporting

We share what we're learning — including limitations — with our community, clinical partners, and the wider field.
